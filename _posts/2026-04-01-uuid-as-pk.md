UUIDv4 doesn't "suck" universally, it just very unfortunate that it's misapplied alot.  
The good part is that it's random, globally unique, good for distributed systems and clients that generate their own ids. The bad part? Database indexes (B+Trees) rely on order, and with UUIDv4's randomness, the database can't reason about this which leads to scattered writes, alot of shuffling and thrashing I/O when writing data.

A more nuanced approach is that... it depends on your workflow, shopify is actually a very good case-study that hit the jackpot with both reads and writes, they switched to ULIDs which are time-ordered and so they went from pulling random pages from disk and this and that to essentially a better approach because of the characteristics of ULIDs, for the case of shopify, this cut operations by 50%, this is massive. Remember, everytime you want to update sth, you need to pull pages into what's called a shared buffer pool, it's an LRU structure that has a limited space, and if you have loosely related random data in the pool, you'll end up with a giant loop of having to evict data from the pool, do an I/O and then evict some more simply because of how your data is scattered, though this only shows up at scale.

Now you might look at this and be like... woooow, omggg 😍😍, not so fast, again, _it depends_, suppose you're building a url shortener, majority of the data you'll have won't be related and therefore you're more likely to not suffer the problems stated in this article (ik, it's much more complex and nuanced than simply this!, databases do have a lot of fancy algorithms and such but this is isn't a deeep dive).
